⏺ Walk-Forward Validation Explained

  Walk-forward validation is a more rigorous way to test trading/prediction models compared to a single fixed train/val/test split. Here's how it works:

  Your Current Approach (Fixed Split)

  2015──────────────2022───2024──2025
  |=================|=====|=====|
      TRAIN (70%)    VAL   TEST
                    (15%) (15%)

  Train once → Validate once → Test once

  You train ONE model on 2015-2022 data, validate on 2022-2024, test on 2024-2025. Done.

  Problem: What if your model just got lucky on the 2022-2024 validation period? Maybe it performs well on that specific market regime but fails on others.

  ---
  Walk-Forward Validation (Multiple Windows)

  Instead of one train/val cycle, you do multiple sequential train/val cycles:

  Window 1:
  2015──2018──2019
  |=====|====|
  TRAIN  VAL

  Window 2:
  2015────2019──2020
  |========|====|
    TRAIN   VAL

  Window 3:
  2015──────2020──2021
  |==========|====|
     TRAIN    VAL

  Window 4:
  2015────────2021──2022
  |============|====|
      TRAIN     VAL

  Window 5:
  2015──────────2022──2023
  |==============|====|
       TRAIN      VAL

  Final Test:
  2015────────────2023──────2025
  |================|==========|
        TRAIN         TEST

  Each window tests the model on a DIFFERENT future period. This proves your model can predict across various market conditions (bull markets, bear markets, crashes, recoveries, etc.).

  ---
  Two Variants

  1. Expanding Window (Recommended for Trading)
  - Training set grows over time
  - 2015-2018 → predict 2018-2019
  - 2015-2019 → predict 2019-2020
  - 2015-2020 → predict 2020-2021
  - Each iteration uses MORE historical data (mimics real trading where you retrain with all history)

  2. Sliding Window
  - Training set size stays fixed
  - 2015-2018 → predict 2018-2019
  - 2016-2019 → predict 2019-2020
  - 2017-2020 → predict 2020-2021
  - Window "slides" forward (mimics using only recent history)

  ---
  Why It's Better for Trading Systems

  1. Tests Multiple Market Regimes
  Your 2015-2025 data includes:
  - 2018: Volatility spike
  - 2020: COVID crash
  - 2021: Bull run
  - 2022: Bear market
  - 2023-2024: Recovery

  Walk-forward proves your model works across ALL these conditions, not just one lucky year.

  2. Reduces Overfitting
  If your model only works on 2022-2024 validation data but fails on other periods, walk-forward catches this. Fixed split might miss it.

  3. Realistic Deployment Simulation
  In live trading, you'd retrain periodically with new data. Walk-forward mimics this:
  - Train on all data up to Dec 2020
  - Trade Jan-Dec 2021
  - Retrain with 2021 data
  - Trade Jan-Dec 2022
  - And so on...

  4. Multiple Performance Estimates
  Instead of ONE validation metric, you get 5+ validation metrics from different periods. You can see:
  - Average performance across periods
  - Worst-case performance
  - Performance consistency
  - Which market regimes your model struggles with

  ---
  The Trade-Off

  Computational Cost:
  - Fixed split: Train 1 model
  - Walk-forward (5 windows): Train 5+ models

  For your CfC model on 10 years of data, walk-forward could take 5-10x longer. But the confidence in your model's robustness is much higher.

  ---
  📚 HYPERPARAMETER EXPLANATIONS - SIMPLE TERMS WITH ANALOGIES

  MODEL ARCHITECTURE

  1. hidden_dim (64)

  What it is: The size of the "compression space" where features are transformed.

  Analogy: Like a notebook where you write study notes
  - Small notebook (64 pages) = Only key bullet points fit
  - Large notebook (256 pages) = Detailed insights and connections

  In your model: Your 582 input features → compressed into 64 key insights per timeframe

  Trade-off:
  - Small (64): Fast, lean, might miss nuances
  - Large (256): Captures complexity, slower, needs more data

  ---
  2. cfc_units (96)

  What it is: Internal size of the Liquid Neural Network (CfC) processor.

  What are Liquid Networks? Instead of discrete steps, they model continuous-time dynamics - like water flowing vs stepping stones.

  Analogy: Water flowing through channels
  - Traditional RNN: Like a train stopping at each station to process
  - Liquid/CfC: Like water continuously flowing and adapting to channel shape

  Why good for trading: Prices move continuously, not in discrete jumps. Channels form smoothly. CfC matches this natural behavior.

  Requirement: Must be > hidden_dim + 2 (you have 96 > 66 ✓)
  Typical ratio: 1.5-2.0x your hidden_dim

  ---
  3. attention_heads (8)

  What it is: Number of "perspectives" looking at your 11 timeframes.

  Analogy: Multiple monitors with different filters
  - Head 1: "Show me trend context" (looks at longer TFs)
  - Head 2: "Show me volatility" (looks at shorter TFs)
  - Head 3: "Show me SPY alignment" (cross-asset focus)
  - Head 4: "Show me RSI extremes" (technical indicator focus)

  Each head learns a different "what to focus on" pattern.

  Why 4-8 heads: With 11 timeframes, you need 2-8 different focus patterns. More than 8 is overkill for time-series.

  ---
  TRAINING PARAMETERS

  4. dropout (0.1)

  What it is: Randomly disables 10% of neurons during training.

  Analogy: Football team practice where random players sit out
  - Forces the team (network) to not rely on any single player
  - Creates redundancy - multiple ways to solve problems
  - Prevents "memorization" of training data

  Values:
  - 0.0 = No protection (can overfit)
  - 0.1 = Light (good default)
  - 0.2 = Moderate
  - 0.5 = Heavy (rare)

  ---
  5. epochs (10)

  What it is: Number of complete passes through all training data.

  Analogy: Reading a textbook
  - 1 epoch = Read it once
  - 10 epochs = Read it 10 times to really learn it
  - 100 epochs = Read it until you memorize every word (might overfit!)

  Typical: 20-100 with early stopping (stops when validation stops improving)

  ---
  6. batch_size (32)

  What it is: How many samples to process before updating weights.

  Analogy: Tasting food while cooking
  - Batch size 1 = Taste after every grain of salt (too reactive)
  - Batch size 32 = Taste after adding several ingredients (balanced)
  - Batch size 1000 = Taste only at the end (too slow to adjust)

  Trade-off:
  - Small (16): More frequent updates, noisier, uses less GPU memory
  - Medium (32-64): Good balance (your setting)
  - Large (128+): Stable, GPU efficient, but fewer updates

  ---
  OPTIMIZATION PARAMETERS

  7. learning_rate (0.001)

  What it is: How big of a step to take when updating weights.

  Analogy: Descending a mountain in fog
  - High LR (0.1) = Giant leaps, might overshoot the bottom
  - Low LR (0.00001) = Tiny steps, takes forever
  - Your setting (0.001) = Measured steps

  Values:
  - 0.0001-0.0005 = Conservative (stable, slow)
  - 0.001-0.01 = Standard
  - 0.1+ = Risky (likely to diverge)

  ---
  8. optimizer: adamw

  What it is: The algorithm that decides HOW to update weights.

  Analogy: Different hiking strategies
  - SGD: Walk straight downhill (simple, reliable, slow)
  - Adam: Run with momentum, adapt stride to terrain (fast, smart)
  - AdamW: Adam + bring lighter backpack (adds weight decay properly)

  Why AdamW: Best of both worlds - adaptive + momentum + regularization

  ---
  9. scheduler: cosine

  What it is: Automatically reduces learning rate over time.

  Analogy: Sculpting
  - Start: Rough chisel (high LR) - big changes
  - Middle: Finer tools (medium LR) - refine shape
  - End: Polishing (low LR) - perfect details

  Cosine = Smooth transition from high to low LR following a cosine curve

  ---
  REGULARIZATION

  10. weight_decay (0.0001)

  What it is: Penalizes large weights to keep model simple.

  Analogy: Employee efficiency review
  - Employees who work TOO hard on specific tasks get penalized
  - Forces them to stay flexible and balanced
  - Prevents overspecialization

  Prevents overfitting by keeping weights small and model patterns general.

  ---
  11. gradient_clipping (1.0)

  What it is: Speed limit on weight updates.

  Analogy: Car governor limiting max speed
  - Without: Can accelerate to 500mph and crash
  - With (limit=1.0): Max safe speed enforced, smooth driving

  Prevents exploding gradients - especially important for CfC/RNNs and financial data.

  LOSS WEIGHTS

  What they control: How much the model cares about each task

  Analogy: Grade weights in school
  Total grade = 40% Math + 20% English + 20% History + 10% Gym

  Math weight is 2x others = model tries harder to get math right
  Gym weight is 0.5x others = model doesn't worry as much about gym

⏺ No, you should NOT have 11 attention heads - that's a common misconception. Here's why:

  Why Number of Heads ≠ Number of Timeframes

  Attention heads and input timeframes serve different purposes:

  Timeframes (11)

  - These are your input positions - what you're attending TO
  - Like having 11 monitors on a trading desk

  Attention Heads (4-8)

  - These are different patterns of importance - HOW you look at those 11 monitors
  - Like having 4 different traders, each with their own strategy:
    - Head 1: "I focus on longer TFs for trend"
    - Head 2: "I focus on shorter TFs for entries"
    - Head 3: "I look for alignment across TFs"
    - Head 4: "I watch for divergences"

  All 4 heads look at the same 11 timeframes, but extract different patterns.

  ---
  Technical Constraint

  You physically CANNOT use 11 heads with hidden_dim=64:

  # Attention requires:
  embed_dim % num_heads == 0

  # Your case:
  64 % 11 = 5.818...  # ❌ Not an integer - won't work!

  # Valid options for hidden_dim=64:
  64 % 1 = 0  ✓  (1 head)
  64 % 2 = 0  ✓  (2 heads)
  64 % 4 = 0  ✓  (4 heads)
  64 % 8 = 0  ✓  (8 heads)
  64 % 16 = 0 ✓  (16 heads)

  Each head needs equal dimensions. With 8 heads, each gets 64/8 = 8 dimensions.

  ---
  Is 8 Heads Good?

  Yes! 8 heads is actually better than 4 for your use case:

  - 4 heads (default): Learns 4 different attention patterns
  - 8 heads (your choice): Learns 8 different attention patterns
    - More expressive
    - Can capture more nuanced relationships between TFs
    - Still efficient (each head only has 8 dims)

  Examples of what 8 heads might learn:
  1. "Focus on daily+ TFs when predicting 5min breaks"
  2. "Focus on 5-30min TFs for short-term volatility"
  3. "Watch for TSLA/SPY alignment"
  4. "Detect RSI extremes across TFs"
  5. "Identify channel nested structures"
  6. "Track bounce patterns"
  7. "Monitor VIX regime changes"
  8. "Detect multi-TF breakouts"

  ---
  Real-World Examples

  - GPT-3: 96 heads for thousands of tokens (not 1 head per token!)
  - Vision Transformers: 12 heads for 196 image patches
  - BERT: 12 heads for 512 token positions
  - Your model: 8 heads for 11 timeframes ✓

  ---
  Bottom Line

  Your choice of 8 heads is excellent:
  - ✅ Divisible by 64 (works technically)
  - ✅ More expressive than default 4
  - ✅ Not overkill like 16 or 32
  - ✅ Good for capturing diverse TF relationships

  The model will automatically learn what patterns to focus on across your 11 timeframes. You don't need 1 head per timeframe!
