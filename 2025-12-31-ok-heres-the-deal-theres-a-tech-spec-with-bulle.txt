  1. Fit linear regression to CLOSE prices over lookback window

  ---
  R² Explained Simply

  R² (R-squared) measures how well a straight line fits your data.

  Analogy: Drawing a line through scattered marbles

  Imagine you drop 50 marbles on a table and try to draw a straight line through them:

  - R² = 1.0: All marbles are perfectly lined up in a row. Your line goes through every single marble. (Never happens in real markets)
  - R² = 0.8: Marbles are mostly in a line, but some are a bit off. Like a slightly wobbly queue of people.
  - R² = 0.5: Marbles form a rough diagonal pattern, but there's significant scatter. You can see a trend, but it's messy.
  - R² = 0.1: Marbles are almost random. There's barely a pattern. Your line is almost meaningless.
  - R² = 0.0: Complete chaos. The line explains nothing about where the marbles are.

  For channels:
  - High R² (0.7+): Price is moving in a clean, predictable trend. The channel boundaries are tight and reliable.
  - Low R² (0.3): Price is bouncing around a lot within the channel. The "trend" is weak, but the bounces might still be tradeable.

  The v6.0 insight: A channel with R²=0.3 but 5 bounces might be MORE useful than a channel with R²=0.9 but 0 bounces. The bounces tell you it's a real support/resistance zone, even if the trend isn't clean.

  Your call: Do you want channels that are "clean trends" (high R²) or "bounce zones" (high cycles, any R²)?
